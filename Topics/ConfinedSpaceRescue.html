What is a confined space?

  • Definition of confined space: An enclosed or partially enclosed space that is not intended or designed primarily for human occupancy, within which there is a risk of one or more of the following
    • an oxygen concentration outside safe oxygen range (19.5-23.5)
    • a concentration of airborne contaminant that may cause impairment, loss of consciousness or asphyxiation
    • a concentration of flammable airborne contaminant that may cause injury from fire or explosion
    • engulfment in a stored free-flowing solid or rising level of liquid that may cause suffocation or drowning
  • a person is considered to have entered a confined space when their head or upper body is within the boundary of the confined space. Reaching into a confined space is not considered entry
  • Hot work is any activity that produced heat, sparks or fire including:
    • Electric welding or cutting, electric grinders, drills or spark producing tools
    • soldering
    • Oxy-welding or cutting (combines oxygen and acetylene gases to produce a high-temperature flame to melt/fuse metals)
    • blast cleaning (as it can produce static sparks)
    • non-flame proof diesel motors, petrol and diesel machinery
  • hot work on surface coatings may produce toxic/flammable by-products, hot work produces fumes hence gas testing must be done before and during work to make sure there are no flammable vapours or gases in the area

Confined space atmosphere

80% of confined space casualties are due to atmosphere, there are 3 main dangers; suffocation, poisoning and explosion

Suffocation

  • Normal O2 is 20.9%, safe range is 19.5-23.5%
  • O2 deficient is deemed as <18% although AS2865:2009 does not allow entry <19.5% without respiratory protection. O2 deficiency can be caused by oxidation of metals, decomposition of organic material combustion or hot work, displacement by another gas (e.g. N2 purged), respiration by other people or animals
  • hypoxia diminishes coordination, clouds thinking, may seem drunk, headache, dizzinesss, nausea, paralysis, collapse, unconsciousness
  • O2 enrichment is >24%, entry prohibited above 23.5%. Higher O2 increases the possibility of fire the increased O2 increases the combustion rate. This enrichment may due to leaking O2 equipment or chemical reactions

Poisoning

Toxic gases may lead to poisoning, exposure standards developed to help people work safely in presence of these gases:
  • Time weighted average (TWA) – average airborne concentration of gas over 8 hour working day
  • Short Term Exposure Level (STEL) – maximum concentration someone can be exposed to for 15mins with a 1hr break, and not more than 4x a day
  • PEAK – max concentration of gas exposed to for period <15mins
  • Immediately dangerous to Life or Health (IDLH) – acute or short term exposures to high concentrations
  • Electronic gas detectors tell you the concentration of various gases in % or ppm and are fitted with alarms which sound at TWA’s and LELs, these detectors should be bump tested and calibrated regulary

Explosion

  • Explosive atmospheres may exist in the presence of flammable contaminants, once mixed with air to support combustion, when the balance is right and the mixture is ignited it can result in explosion.
  • Lower explosive limit (LEL) – lowest gas concentration that can lead to an explosion in presence of air and ignition source. To permit entry to work in confined space, the %contaminant must be <5% of the LEL, if it raises during work it should ventilated and monitored, evacuation should occur at 10% of LEL. Rescues can be performed up to 10% of LEL
  • Upper Explosive Limit (UEL) – highest concentration of gas that can lead to explosion in presence of air and ignition source

Testing and making the atmosphere safe

  • Due to different densities of gases, stratification can occur within the confined space, therefore gas monitoring should be performed at all levels and all entry/exit points
  • If hot work is performed or if the space itself may give off contaminants, then continuous monitoring should be performed, otherwise at least at 15min intervals or as required
  • Ventilation
    • Natural ventilation – relies on wind, natural dispersion, requires 2 openings on opposite ends of the space, best for low risk spaces
    • Mechanical, positive pressure ventilation – forces clean air in using fans and ducting
    • Mechanical, negative pressure ventilation – extracts contaminated air out using fans and ducting
    • Purging agents – inert gases such as nitrogen pumped into confined space to displace harmful gases. Oxygen must NOT bed used as it increases the range of flammability (reduces LEL, increases UEL)
    • AS2865:2009 prohibits compressed gas cylinders from being brought into confined spaces except compressed air
