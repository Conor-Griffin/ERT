8 stages of RCR operation (PRIGEDRT)

  1. Preparation - Regular training, equipment maintenance, and familiarization with Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs).
  2. Response - Safe travel to the scene, assessing the approach, effective positioning of appliance
    • Check ANCAP rescue app for specific vehicle info such as locations of battery, airbags, gas struts, pre-tensioners
    • Fend off position – protects incident site and rescue crews, if at night, appliance should also be parked to maximise lighting on scene
  3. Incident management – Scene assessment (inner and outer circles), hazard control, and establishing vehicle rescue zones, preserving scene
    • Rescue scene reconnaissance, check weather, equipment required
    • Inner circle: search the vehicle and area at a distance of up to 5m, identify number of casualties, type of injuries and entrapment, access, operational hazards
    • Action circle ~3-5m from incident, tool staging area should be on edge of action circle. There should also be a dedicated parts dump area
    • Outer circle: look up to 30m away for potential further casualties
    • Hazards could include irrespirable atmospheres, bodily fluids, difficult terrain, exposed electrical conductors, HAZMAT, fuel-fire, airbags, sharp edges, damaged power lines etc
    • Battery is an ignition source, should be disconnected to prevent fire, unwanted airbag activation. Vehicles with centralised locking should have doors/boot unlocked and electric windows and seats should be wound down and adjusted PRIOR to isolation if possible. Remove the negative (black) terminal by loosening nut with wrench, pull the clamp off, secure cable to prevent accidental reconnection then remove pos terminal. (current flows from positive to negative, negative terminal is the return terminal of car’s chassis, removing positive (red) could cause short circuit if tool contacts metal part of car). If battery cannot be disconnected due to nature of crash, keys must be removed from ignition, but there will still be energy stored for a ~15 minutes.
    • Charged Fire hose or extinguisher should be placed close to the engine bay ASAP.
    • High voltage cables are coloured bright orange, assume live. SRS (airbag) wiring is yellow or orange, don’t cut
    • Seat belt pretensioners contain a pyrotechnic charge/spring that could pose a risk if cut
  4. Gain access - Executing rapid and safe access to casualties (e.g., through doors or windows) and stabilizing the vehicle.
    • Stabilise via chains, winch, chocking, jacking, ratchet straps, ropes, webbing
    • Stabilise vehicles still on 4 wheels by chocking, use hammer to wedge blocks in, but be mindful not to shake the car too much as it could worsen condition of casualty
    • For rollovers, cutting roof sheet metal may be the easiest way of gaining access/removing casualty
    • First aid measures such as lifting and holding the chin of an unconscious casualty may allow them to breath again
    • Comfort casualty if conscious, walk them through what is happening
  5. Emergency care - Administering first aid using the DRSABCD method
    • Use DRSABCD, then assess nature/cause of injuries, there may be blood on steering wheel or ‘spider web’ crack pattern on glass
    • Front collision may cause pelvis, hip, femur, sternum fractures
    • Side impact elbow, humerus, clavicle, fracture, shoulder dislocation, head injuries, cervical spine injuries
    • Possible internal bleeding
    • Rollover – cervical and thoracic spinal injuries, head injuries
    • Rear impact – neck sprain/whiplash, cervical spinal injury
    • All may cause broken ribs/ flail chest
    • Possible complications such as ruptured lung, haemothorax, laceration of the great vessels, pressure on the heart (pericardial tamponade)
  6. Disentanglement - The physical process of freeing the trapped person using techniques like disassembly, displacement, distortion, or severance of the vehicle
    • Prior to cutting/spreading, do a ‘peek and peel’ – use flathead screwdriver to check for airbags, airbag inflators (metal tubes), seat belt pre tensioners, gas struts to identify where not to cut. Gas struts of boot may be removed with flathead screwdriver.
    • Tools: Hydraulic pump, cutter, spreader, ram, hooligan tool, reciprocating saw, drills (undo bolts for disassembly)
    • Door opening:
      1. Attempt to open doors normally prior to using tools
      2. Use spreaders to crimp door or spread door/roof to open an entry point for spreaders near the door lock, can also peel outer sheet metal back with hooligan tool to expose entry point for spreaders
    • Roof fold: increases room
      1. Remove windscreen
      2. Cut through A pillars, and other pillars as required whilst supporting roof
      3. Cutting each side of roof at the fold point
      4. Placing crowbar/rod across fold point
      5. Folding roof back and securing in place
    • Roof removal: Similar to roof fold but cut all pillars and cut seat belts from attachment points. Seatbelts should be pulled taught, cut at 45deg angle down
    • Dash roll: for trapped front seat occupant
      1. Remove the windscreen
      2. Cut both A pillars, cut at base A of pillars/corner
      3. Placing ram from base of B pillar to top hinge of A pillar
      4. Extending ram, and chocking the gap so it doesn’t fall back once ram is removed/slips
  7. Removal and transfer – removing casualty from vehicle and transferring to ambulance
    • Remove them safely, prevent further injury
    • Can use stretchers, spine boards or KED (Kendrick extrication device)
    • KED is used to immobilize a seated patient’s neck, head and torso, preventing possible further spinal injury
  8. Termination - Concluding the operation, including final checks, scene cleanup, equipment recovery, and operational debriefing
    • Preserve incident for police – don’t disturb fatalities, take photos prior to moving things
    • Equipment recovery – checking condition, functionality, discarding/retiring equipment, maintenance
    • Decontamination
    • Debriefing, documentation (contemporaneous notes)

Glass management

  • Windows should be wound down prior to battery isolation possible to capture glass within door
  • Plastic shields should be used to cover patient prior to smashing glass on near side, and also between patient and extrication tools
  • Magnetic blue ‘jaws of life’ mats can be used as interior shielding or to cover sharp edges prior to casualty removal
  • P2 masks should be worn (by casualty too if possible) when cutting front windscreen glass due to potential irritation from glass dust
  • Side and rear windows use toughened/tempered glass which shatters with a light blow from a small object. Whereas front windscreen is laminated glass (which is 2 layers of glass bonded to a tough plastic interlayer film) will require a solid blow to break, and needs to be cut out with glass saw or recip saw.
